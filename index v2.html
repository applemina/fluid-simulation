FLUID MECHANICS · GRAVITY FLUSH DEMO

What does it take to get your shit down?

A gravity-flush toilet is a three-stage fluid system: a tank stores hydrostatic energy, a valve converts it into a jet, and a ring of rim holes distributes that jet around the bowl to scour it clean. Tune the tank, the valve, the liquid, and the rim geometry below to see whether the flush actually clears the bowl or just dribbles and fails.

Energy Source — Tank & Flush Valve
Hydrostatic pressure and Bernoulli/Torricelli flow through the orifice

Hydrostatic pressure: P = ρgH — a taller water column pushes harder on the valve (live substitution shown on the right). Bernoulli / Torricelli: ideal exit speed v = √(2gH), reduced by a viscosity-dependent discharge coefficient Cd as the liquid gets thicker (water → soap solution → gel cleaner) and the orifice Reynolds number drops. Tank is fixed at 12 cm × 50 cm, matching a real cistern's proportions.

Distribution Network — Rim & Holes
Flow splits across N rim holes; each jet imposes wall shear on the ceramic

Conservation of flow: water enters the rim channel at a single point at the top (start) and discharges through each hole in turn, so the flow — and velocity — remaining in the channel decreases monotonically as it travels around the rim (see the fading ring color and the start/end channel-velocity readouts). Rim cavity sets the channel's own cross-section: a narrower cavity means higher channel velocity for the same Q. Hagen–Poiseuille wall shear: τwall = 4μvhole/rhole at each exit jet. Reynolds number (gauge, right) tells you the flow regime at the rim jets — see the three zones below.
